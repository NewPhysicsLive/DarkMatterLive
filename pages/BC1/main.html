Description of the model

In the minimal dark photon framework, the Standard Model (SM) is extended by an additional \(\mathrm{U}(1)_D\) gauge symmetry under which all SM particles are neutral. The new gauge field, typically denoted \(A'_\mu\), mixes kinetically with the SM hypercharge gauge field \(B_\mu\). The most compact Lagrangian capturing this minimal extension reads:

\[ \mathcal{L} \;\supset\; -\tfrac14\,F'_{\mu\nu}F'^{\mu\nu} + \tfrac12\,m_{A'}^2\,A'_\mu A'^\mu + \epsilon\,e\,A'^\mu J_{\mu}^{\mathrm{EM}} \, , \]

where \(F'_{\mu\nu}\) is the dark photon field strength, \(m_{A'}\) is its mass (generation might be not specified, commonly accepted a Stueckelberg or dark Higgs mechanism), and \(\epsilon\) parametrizes the strength of kinetic mixing with the electromagnetic current \(J_{\mu}^{\mathrm{EM}} = \sum_f Q_f \, \bar{f} \gamma_\mu f\).

The dark photon originates from the new \(\mathrm{U}(1)_D\) gauge symmetry. If \(\mathrm{U}(1)_D\) remains unbroken, the dark photon is massless and its interactions with the SM are extremely suppressed. More commonly, the symmetry is broken (e.g., via the Stueckelberg or dark Higgs mechanism), leading to a massive dark photon. The two central free parameters of the minimal model are \(m_{A'}\) and \(\epsilon \).

Detection

There are two types of channels that are usually considered: visible (\(m_{A'} > 2 m_e \)) and invisible (\(m_{A'} < 2 m_e\)).

In the first case, seaches concentrated on detection of SM signatures related to the dark photon in detector, e.g., dijet and missing energy/momentum searches. These experiments are usually built around colliders, like LHC, SPS, Fermilab etc.

In the second case, the dark photon, in minimal dark photon model, do not have obvious visible SM decay channels, exept neutrinos, which are suppressed and extremely hard to detect, and so-called photon trident decay (\(A' \rightarrow 3 \gamma \)), which is one-loop processes and highly suppressed. Therefore, these particles are long-lived and escape the detector, leading to missing energy/momentum signatures, which are extremely weak compared to the background and imposible to track at current resolution.

So, most seaches at sub-MeV range are based on indirect detection methods.

Indirect detection methods include astrophysical observations, cosmological measurements, and precision experiments. The first two methods look for deviations from the expected behavior of astrophysical objects or cosmological phenomena that could be explained by the presence of dark photons. These methods often use strong assumptions, like \(\rho_{DM} = \rho_{A'}\) and are model-dependent, so they often impose less strict restrictins, compared to model indepent constraints, and should be treat with caution. Precision experiments, on the other hand, can provide robust constraints on dark photons by looking for tiny deviations from the SM predictions (expecially, Coulomb force) in laboratory settings.

In the context of dark photons, direct detection methods are almost interchangeable with collider experiments, as any signature of dark photons in colliders would be considered a direct detection of them.

Let's consider our proposed clasification of experiments in the context of dark photon searches:

By detection type:

  • Direct detection: related directly to interaction with dark matter, including collider experiments looking for visible decays of dark photons (e.g., \(A' \rightarrow e^+ e^-\)) or missing energy/momentum signatures, wall experiments, haloscopes;
  • Indirect detection: Astrophysical observations (e.g., stellar cooling, supernovae), cosmological measurements (e.g., CMB, BBN), and precision experiments (e.g., anomalous magnetic moments, Coulomb force).

By experiment type:

  • Collider experiments: all experimets that are connected with accelerators, including collider experimets (e.g., LHCb, BaBar, CMS), fixed-target experiments (e.g., NA64, APEX, HPS), and beam dump experiments (e.g., E137, E774, Orsay, CHARM);
  • Laboratory experiments: all Earth-based experiments without colliders, including precision experiments (e.g, spectroscopy, Plimpton-Lawton experiment), wall experiments (e.g, CROWS, ALPS, SPring-8), haloscopes (e.g., AMAILS).
  • Astrophysical observations: Supernova 1987A, helioscopes etc.
  • Cosmological measurements: Cosmic Microwave Background (CMB) anisotropies (e.g., Planck, COBE, FIRAS), Big Bang Nucleosynthesis (BBN) etc.

By time of the results:

  • Past constraints: before 2015 (e.g., E137, E141, E774, Orsay, CHARM);
  • Recent constraints: after 2015 due to today (e.g., LHCb, BaBar, NA64);
  • Planned/future constraints: in future (e.g., Belle II, APEX, HPS, PADME).

By used assumptions

  • None\(^*\): model-independent constraints (e.g., collider experiments, precision experiments);
  • Dark matter: constraints assuming dark photon constitutes all or part of dark matter (e.g., astrophysical observations, cosmological measurements);
  • Other: constraints based on specific theoretical models.

* "None” does not mean that there are no assumptions at all. In fact, many simplifying assumptions are always made in the calculations. Rather, it means that these assumptions do not require dark particles to be embedded in a broader theoretical framework, where the validity of the results would entirely depend on the correctness of that model.
